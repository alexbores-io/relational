why

Why this matters

The NY-12 Democratic primary is on June 23, 2026. Primaries like this are typically decided by a few hundred to a few thousand votes — turnout is low, persuasion is tractable, and most people haven't thought about it yet.

Collectively, our communities know thousands of registered Democrats in NY-12. If a few hundred of us each have a real conversation with 3–5 friends, we can plausibly move a hundred-plus votes. In a race this size, that's material.

The main ask: relational organizing

Roughly two to five hours of your time across six weeks, doing the following:

  • Find 3–5 people you know in NY-12. We'll show you Reach, an app that cross-references your contacts against the voter file.
  • Message them. Text, call, whatever's natural. Tell them why you care about Bores.
  • Ask if they'll vote. Help them make a concrete plan — "I'll early-vote Saturday morning" beats "yeah, probably."
  • Follow up. Once closer to election day, a short check-in.
  • Log it so we can tell who's already been contacted — the goal is one friend reaching out, not ten.

You don't have to do anything aversive. If you want to do more, we'll find things.

Who should reach out to friends?

  • People in New York City — more latitude, it's not weird to organize politically in your own city.
  • People outside New York — only for contacts you'd already text casually. Don't cold-message someone you haven't spoken to in five years; that reads as weird and is counterproductive.

Other ways to help

Relational outreach isn't for everyone, and not everyone knows people in NY-12. Plenty of other useful things:

If you're in NY-12 or NYC

  • Canvassing with the campaign
  • Phone or text banking off campaign-provided lists
  • Poll greeting on election day (Jun 23) or during early voting (Jun 13–22)
  • Showing up at events — visibility helps

If you're anywhere

  • Donating to the campaign (FEC limits apply)
  • Remote phone or text banking — works from anywhere
  • Skills — design, dev, writing, ops. If you do this professionally and want to contribute, read the FEC note below first
  • Thoughtful amplification online — substantive posts from people who know the candidate beat generic "vote for X" spam

faq

Concerns, addressed

Is this legal / okay with campaign finance rules?

Volunteer time is fine. Spending money on this effort (merch, paid ads, etc.) can count as an in-kind contribution and needs to be reported — talk to us before spending. Professional services (design, engineering, etc.) done for the campaign need to be at market rate unless you do it as a genuine volunteer for something unrelated to your profession. None of this is legal advice; if you have a specific question, ask a lawyer.

What's the campaign's involvement?

The campaign provides guidance on messaging and best practices. They don't manage this effort, and we don't speak for them.

Can I talk to the media about this?

Please don't, by default. If you're getting press questions, forward them to us and we'll connect you to someone appropriate.

What if Bores loses?

He might — this is a competitive primary. We're being upfront about that. The case for trying is that a few hours of your time has a real probability of moving the outcome, and either way you'll have had honest conversations with people you care about.

How do you keep contacts from being messaged by ten different people?

There's a shared tracker of who's reached out to whom. When you sign up, you get assigned specific contacts and we coordinate to avoid the pile-on problem.

What if I don't want to message friends?

Totally fine — plenty of other ways to help, see the list above. Mention it in the email and we'll skip straight to something else.
